On Gender studies

Gender is ubuitous and ever-existing. Even in places where it is not considered, it plays a significant role.

In places, where freedom is under constant attack, it is all more relevant to speak out and to show that support exists.

So please, feel free to examine this website, and learn all about gender issues in the large country far up North, Russian Federation.

How it all
Begins
The goal of this website is to discuss feminism and gender rights in Russia. For a country with a long and complex history, there always is a long and complicated story of treatment of women, minorities, and LGBTQ community.
The topics discussed here intersect and intertwine, and each of them deserves a separate discussion. The goal of this website is not to create an exhaustive list, but rather create interest spark interest in readers. Our goal is to show the diversity and struggle of communities in Russia, and although in many cases the story behind it is sad and discouraging, we believe the history can change it, and our action is directed to help this change.
Please feel free to explore the sections of the website in any order, as well as share the link to the website with anyone you would like to share it. You can do it using the the "share" icon on the top-right of the screen.
Then...
In this section we would like to discuss the history of women in Russia. We will also briefly discuss the history of minorities, and LGBTQ community in Russia.
When discussing poetry in Russia, it is impossible to omit the women writers that are especially prevalent in the poetry of the "Silver Age". People like Marina Tsvetayeva, Anna Akhmatova, Mirra Lokhvitskaya were essential poets of the early 20th century Russian Empire and Soviet Union, and their legacy is often overlooked in the World (although students do learn about most of these poets to some extent at schools in Russia).
20th century is a turbulent time in history of Russia. From getting the votes rights in 1917 to the tightening of marriage laws and "population transfers" of minorities and "enemies of the people" in 1930, with the constant changes of the government course, the human rights were not achieved easily, yet easily lost. To this day, the rules of the past affect people of Russia and countries-parts of the Soviet Union.
... and Now
Today, the rights of women and minorities are constantly under attack in Russia. Decriminalization of domestic violence, withdrawal of native language education from the school curriculums in regions of Russia, abduction, torture and imprisonment of people based on their sexual orientation (prevalent in Chechen Republic). Those are just a few examples of the complicated human rights situtation today.
Today's Russia is going backwards in terms of progress of human rights. This, however, doesn't mean that there is no hope. There are still resources, movements, activists who bravely continue to protect the integral rights of humans in Russia. We recommend readers to see the resources provided in "Learn More" and "There is Help" sections to learn more about that.
An important part of discussion is the representation of the cultural minorities in Russia. There is very little attention in Russian schools curriculum dedicated to cultures of minorities, though they take up to 30% of the population by some measurements. Some of the biggest cultural minorities in Russia are: Tatars, Chechens, Bashkirs, Circassians, Yakuts, Udmurts, Nenets.
As an exercise, we propose readers to pick one of these nationalities and read an article or watch a video on their culture. You will be surprised how much you can learn just from one of these cultures!
War
It is impossible to talk about Russia today without mentioning war.
War has changed millions of lives of Ukranians and Russians, and the consequences of this war can be seen across the whole globe. We believe it is important now more than ever to not stay quiet about this, and continue talking about the devastations that war brings with it daily.
People of Ukraine suffer, and that is directly related to the topic of this website: human rights.
War brought severe restrictions inside Russian society as well. Several laws have been passed regulating LGBTQ community, and more hate crimes have been observed towards LGBTQ compared to the past years. War brings sorrow, and sorrow grows inside human minds, poisoning clear thinking of those who still had it before.
Helping Ukraine to end this war means helping people of Russia to get back the numerous rights that have been taken from them.

Share
This is a part of the website where we would like to refer to some of the material that was used in creation of this website. The material that was used was primarily written in Russian, although We encourage our readers to check these links, even if they don't speak one of the languages, because we believe the resources provided can be helpful not only in their content, but in the way the content is presented there.
Burning Hut (Горящая Изба) (in Russian)
This website is dedicated to Women's Rights and "everything that is interesting to women today". It is a brilliantly designed website that provided a lot of information on domestic violence, gender inequality, and other topics that were covered here. It is important to mention that the material of the material is not limited by these topics, and covers a lot of everyday aspects, such as pop-culture, news, and business.
Arzamas (primarily in Russian, with some articles in English)
The website is dedicated to social sciences, including history, literature, sociology, as well as cultural science (including articles on cultures of minorities).
